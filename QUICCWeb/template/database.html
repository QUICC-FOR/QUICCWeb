QUICC-Base

At continental scale, a large amount of data...

The QUICC-FOR database contains permanent and temporary forest inventory sample plots from United States and Canada. The data has been freely provided by partners and covers 3 eastern Canadian provinces (app. 18,000 plots) and 31 states of eastern USA ( app. 150,000 plots). Data is recorded for seedlings, trees, saplings and stand level. All inventories are geo-referenced. Climatic variables are associated to each plot by interpolating the climatic model ANUSPLIN from Natural Ressources Canada.

At forest stand scale, three forest permanent plots...

Establishment of these plots has started in 2009 and we will conduct a sampling of growth increment cores to collect fine-scale radial growth data. The three plots are located at the transition between temperate and boreal forests (Estrie, Bas-St-Laurent and Abitibi). The main plot is located at Sutton (Estrie, 20 Ha) along a 200 meters elevation gradient, so that the tree composition shifts from temperate broadleaf at the bottom to coniferous at the top. The other two plots (one near Rimouski further north and the other in Temiscamingue, further north- west) are smaller (5 Ha) and located in homogeneous environmental conditions, but have considerable spatial variability in species composition. Within each of those permanent plots, each tree location (up to 10 cm of DBH) has been recorded.

Sites description

Abitibi permanent plot

The first of the study sites lies within the Abitibi-Temiscaminque region of western Quebec (48° 9'45.14"N; 79°24'4.39"W). The site is located in the north-eastern temperate zone and is at the northern limit of the mixed hardwood forest subzone. The mixed forest subzone is primary found between 47° and 48° latitude and encompasses an area of approximately 98,600 km2. Sugar maple is found to be at the northernmost limit within this range, where the primary tree species within this zone includes: balsam fir (Abies balsamea), yellow birch (Betual alleghaniensis), white spruce (Picea glauca), and white cedar (Thuja occidentalis). The research site is of particular interest as study plot since it has no record of any significant fire; the primary natural disturbance of the region. Site topography of the site is generally flat and uniform with elevations ranging between 350m and 400m above sea level. The site is located within the clay belt: a large physiographic region that was created by lacustrine deposits from proglacial lakes Barlo and Ojibway within Ontario and Québec. Mean annual temperatures were taken (1994-2012) from the Rouyn field station (~35km from study site) and were are found to be -15.4°C in January and 18.0°C in July. Annual precipitation over this same measurement period was found to be on average 868 mm.

Bas-Saint-Laurent permanent plot

The second of the research sites is located within the Bic National Park (48°20'1.03"N; 68°49'3.79"W). Recognized as a national park (category II) by the International Union for Consevation of Nature (IUCN) in 1984, it is now managed by the Société des établissements de plein air du Québec (SÉPAQ) since 1999. Prior to park status and more commonly in the 19th century, the forested area had been used as a woodlot and was frequently selective harvested by local inhabitants. The site is located within the mixedwood forest subzone, which is a part of the north- eastern temperate zone. Similarly to the Abitibi site, sugar maple is at its northernmost limit within this zone. However, the frequency of yellow birch trees significantly drops from the west in favour for balsam fir in this eastern section of the mixed forest subzone. The site elevation generally ranges between 200m and 320m above sea level and is located on a ridge, where part of the plot is located on a rugged flat surface and the other part exposed to a steep slope facing south. The main soil deposit on site is a glacier till with pockets of organic soil in local depressions, which ranges from rocky exposure to deep till >1m. Mean annual temperatures taken (1953-2012) from the Rimouski field station (~20km from study site) range from -11.1°C for January to 18.0°C in July, and possess a mean annual precipitation of 892 mm.

Estrie permanent plot

The third research site is located in the Appalachian-Green Mountain range of the eastern townships of Québec; ~6 km east of the town of Sutton (45° 6'46.09"N; 72°32'28.67"W). Designated a nature reserve in 2001 when the Nature Conservancy of Canada purchased 460Ha (Note: the reserve is now approximately 6785 Ha in size), the permanent plot is located along an elevation gradient, which ranges from approximately 400m to 850m above sea level. The site is classified by Ministère des ressources naturelles (2003) as part of the northern temperate zone and sugar maple-basswood (Tilia americana) subzone, which covers an estimated 31,000km2 area (Figure 4). The site is predominately classified as possessing a glacial till with large pockets of organic material. Median annual temperatures taken (1979-2012) at the Sutton weather field station were found to be -9.2°C in January and 19.7°C in July, while annual precipitation over this same time period was 1314mm.
